An argument for urbanism

I began my life at Shepherd as a student for two semesters before moving off campus so I can speak for both sides when I say that it feels almost impossible to walk anywhere on campus without gearing up for a hike. Sure, I like parking closer to my classes, but ideally, I should be able to park across campus and walk or bike across in a reasonable time with relative comfort and safety. Let’s say I have a disability and the parking spaces downtown are all taken so I have to park at Potomac and walk through the underpass to get to class. It’s not a huge and unmanageable distance, but there’s very little accommodation for those with disabilities to safely and comfortably get to campus due to the significant inclines and declines. I understand that hill grades may not be a big deal for most people, but for those with disabilities, they can literally prevent them from getting places safely, comfortably, and on time without driving directly there. Thankfully, some of this is being addressed in Shepherd University’s current master plan.

Shepherd University's master plan outlines the university's objectives and goals for campus development, proposing a ten-year implementation path that identifies new facilities to address near and long-term goals while acknowledging the value of the campus's historic buildings. The university's location on the Potomac River in a historic community within close proximity to Maryland, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and Washington D.C. provides a unique opportunity for campus development that takes into account its unique location and history while addressing its near and long-term goals. Overall, Shepherd University's master plan provides a framework for future campus development that takes into account its unique location and history while also addressing its near and long-term goals.

On page 14 of the master plan, it is shown that while there is technically sufficient parking overall for students and faculty, the east campus contains a slim minority of the parking. This causes issues when commuters and faculty who do not live on campus prefer parking closer to the buildings they are working or going to classes in. Many may say that the solution to this would be to increase parking on the east side of campus, and while that may help somewhat, I argue that increasing campus accessibility and foot traffic is much more important than an increase in parking because it would decrease the need for motor vehicles in and around campus by encouraging more bike use as well as allowing all on-campus students to safely get to class without a vehicle even in adverse conditions.

Shepherd University is located in historic Shepherdstown, West Virginia, and is neatly tucked one block behind downtown German Street. Because of this, the University has a unique opportunity to create a more pedestrian-friendly campus by increasing pedestrianization and reducing the emphasis on parking. One of the key benefits of increased pedestrianization is that it can create a more welcoming and attractive campus environment. This would not only benefit current students, faculty, and staff but also attract prospective students and visitors to the University. In contrast, increasing parking on campus would not have the same positive impact on campus aesthetics. Parking lots take up valuable space that could be used for green spaces or other amenities that enhance campus life. Additionally, large parking lots can create heat islands and contribute to stormwater runoff problems.

But, while locals may walk around every day to their local cafes, jobs, and parks, this is not a realistic vision for many of Shepherd’s commuters who make up around 69% of the student as of 2022 (https://www.shepherd.edu/quick-facts/), not to mention the amount of staff who drive to campus. Commuters typically live off campus and commute to campus, a majority by driving unless they live near the downtown area (I say this because I’m still in Shepherdstown technically but it would be extremely unsafe for me to walk that distance down Martinsburg pike, let alone the street I live on). Because of this, putting funds into increasing walkability could be seen as a waste of resources when they could be spent demolishing unsafe buildings and putting new parking in for those who require it. However, these concerns can be addressed by implementing alternative transportation options such as shuttle services or bike-sharing programs. Additionally, creating more walkable and bikeable routes to campus can encourage more people to choose active transportation modes instead of driving. As Jane Jacobs wrote in “The Death and Life of Great American Cities,” “There is a quality even meaner than outright ugliness or disorder, and this meaner quality is the dishonest mask of pretended order, achieved by ignoring or suppressing the real order that is struggling to exist and to be served” (Jacobs). By considering the needs and desires of all members of the community, including commuters and staff, we can create a more inclusive and sustainable transportation system.

Furthermore, it is important to consider the long-term benefits of investing in sustainable transportation options. While it may seem more practical in the short term to focus on increasing car access and parking availability, this approach is not sustainable in the long run. By prioritizing walkability, bikeability, and public transportation, Shepherdstown can reduce its carbon footprint and improve the overall quality of life for its residents and visitors. This approach aligns with Jane Jacobs’ belief that “We expect too much of new buildings, and too little of ourselves” (Jacobs). By taking responsibility for our own actions and making conscious choices about how we move around our community, we can create a more sustainable and livable future.

Also included in the master plan was a new dining hall and multiple residence halls, these were thought to provide a large boost to the student population, but due to the pandemic and the closing of other residence halls, Fall enrollment and head counts have stagnated and even declined in recent years. It would be important to note that an increase in students would require an increase in sufficient amenities to accommodate the larger population. The Plan mentions a new student center on the east campus to ease this issue, as well as attaching a parking garage to it for increased parking for commuters and faculty. Other changes, though, would have a much more drastic effect on not only campus design but community culture. For example, the pedestrianization of King Street.

The pedestrianization of King Street would be a major change to the Shepherd campus and a needed one at that. King Street is extremely steep and hostile to pedestrians. The street within the university grounds could be much better served as a communal walking space to host events and encourage student involvement. Another benefit of increased pedestrianization is improved safety for pedestrians. By reducing vehicle traffic on campus, there would be fewer conflicts between vehicles and pedestrians. This would make it safer for students, faculty, staff, and visitors to walk or bike around campus. Hopefully, this new footpath would also include stairs down to the river, as that has been needed by the community for many years now. The only alternative routes down to the river trail are streets with no sidewalks and for some, it's essential to drive purely for your own safety.

Another priority stated in the master plan is the strengthening of campus identity. As far as campus identity goes, I feel like it’s pretty clear that we’re here, however, I will admit that Shepherdstown itself has a very strong brand, so the university could probably use some stronger branding of its own. A solution to this would be the creation of a new gateway to the East Campus and the pedestrianization of King Street as they would both enhance the overall aesthetic appeal of the campus (Shepherd University - King Street Pedestrian & Streetscape Improvements · Design Collective). The addition of a parking garage, in contrast, is somewhat gross in my eyes because of all the nearby greenery. However, on the map on page 25 of the master plan it seems to be hidden well by the proposed student center which would be highly beneficial. As Turner notes, “colleges with less areas to build on, sometimes structure their buildings around vertical, rather than a horizontal social space” (291). By utilizing vertical space and carefully considering the placement of new buildings, Shepherd University can enhance its campus identity while preserving its natural beauty.

In writing this essay, I carefully considered the sources of evidence that would best support my arguments and claims about increasing urbanization in Shepherdstown. To gather evidence, I turned to a variety of sources, including books by renowned urban theorists such as Jane Jacobs and Miles Orvell, as well as campus planning documents from Shepherd University and other institutions. I also consulted the Shepherd University website for up-to-date information about the university’s plans and initiatives. By drawing on a diverse range of sources, I was able to gather strong evidence to support my arguments and provide a well-rounded perspective on the topic.

In conclusion, increasing pedestrianization on Shepherd University’s Campus should be prioritized over increasing parking. A more pedestrian-friendly campus would enhance its aesthetic appeal and improve safety for pedestrians. In contrast, large parking lots can detract from campus aesthetics and contribute to environmental problems. By focusing on creating a more welcoming and attractive campus environment through increased pedestrianization, Shepherd University can enhance the overall campus experience for students, faculty, staff, and visitors.
